How We Got Here

Estimated cumulative US direct spending tied to the Iran conflict, from January 2025 through the launch of Operation Epic Fury in February 2026. Events marked with red dots represent major US military actions; gold dots represent significant escalation milestones.

Note on sourcing: Events through June 2025 are documented real occurrences with primary sources linked below each entry. Events from September 2025 onward reflect available public reporting through the site's anchor date of March 2, 2026. Cost figures are estimates based on confirmed weapons unit costs, known force deployments, and published analyses. See Sources & Methodology for full detail.
April 20, 2024 — Background Context
$95B Foreign Aid Package — $26.4B for Israel Security (H.R.8034)
Congress passed H.R.8034, the Israel Security Supplemental Appropriations Act, 2024, allocating $26.38 billion for Israel as part of a $95 billion combined package (also including $60.8B for Ukraine). Key components: $4B to replenish Iron Dome and David's Sling; $3.5B in Foreign Military Financing; $4.4B to replenish US war reserve stocks drawn down to supply Israel. Weapons drawdowns from this authorization continued into 2025 and directly funded systems deployed in the Iran conflict.
Israel allocation: $26.38B · Ongoing FY2025 drawdowns included in this tracker
Congress.gov H.R.8034 CBO Cost Estimate PBS Breakdown
October 1, 2024 — Escalation Marker
Iran fires ~180–200 ballistic missiles at Israel ("Operation True Promise II")
Iran launched approximately 180–200 ballistic missiles in a single salvo at Israel — a significant escalation from its April 2024 attack. US forces intercepted multiple missiles alongside Israeli air defenses. The US military's interception of Iranian missiles during this attack depleted interceptor stockpiles, setting the stage for the costly replenishment programs that would follow in 2025. The IDF cited ~180 missiles; the White House cited "more than 200."
US interceptor costs (SM-3, THAAD): est. $200–400M · Full cost not included in 2025 tracker
Wikipedia NPR Report Al Jazeera
March 15, 2025
FY2025 Full-Year Appropriations Signed — $3.3B Foreign Military Financing for Israel
President Trump signed H.R.1968, the Full-Year Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act, 2025, which funds Israel's baseline Foreign Military Financing at $3.3 billion — the statutory annual security assistance level established by US–Israel MOU. The act also includes $450.3M for Israeli Off-Shore Procurement and ongoing missile defense replenishment. This is separate from and in addition to the emergency April 2024 supplemental.
FY2025 FMF: $3.3B · Plus $450M offshore procurement
Congress.gov H.R.1968 CRS Report RL33222
March–May 2025
Operation Rough Rider — Sustained US Airstrikes on Iran-Proxy Houthi Forces in Yemen
The Trump administration authorized an expanded, sustained air campaign against Houthi military infrastructure in Yemen. US forces conducted multiple weeks of carrier-based and B-2 Spirit strikes from Diego Garcia, targeting Houthi weapons depots, missile launch sites, and command nodes. Houthis are designated as a US-sanctioned Iranian proxy that had disrupted global shipping since October 2023. The Pentagon sought emergency supplemental appropriations to sustain the operational tempo. Costs include over 200 Tomahawk cruise missiles (~$2M each), B-2 sorties, and carrier air wing operations.
Estimated total Operation Rough Rider cost: $1.5–2.5B · Iran-proxy conflict costs included in this tracker
CSIS Analysis Breaking Defense
June 13–24, 2025
The Twelve-Day War — Iran vs. Israel: US depletes 14% of THAAD stockpile
Israel launched surprise airstrikes on Iranian military and nuclear facilities on June 13, 2025 ("Operation Rising Lion"). Iran retaliated with 550+ ballistic missiles and 1,000+ drones over 12 days. US forces intercepted Iranian attacks, expending approximately 92 THAAD interceptors at ~$12.7M each — roughly 14% of the total US THAAD stockpile of ~632 interceptors. Additional SM-3, Patriot PAC-3, and SM-6 expenditures brought total US air defense costs to $2.7–4.7B for the 12-day window (JINSA, July 2025). The Pentagon warned restocking will take 3–8 years at current production rates.
THAAD interceptors: 92 × $12.7M = $1.17B · Total US air defense costs: $2.7–4.7B (JINSA)
Wikipedia: Twelve-Day War JINSA: Insights JINSA Cost Report (PDF)
June 22, 2025
Operation Midnight Hammer — First direct US strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities
US forces struck Iran's three main nuclear facilities at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. Seven B-2 Spirit stealth bombers departed Diego Garcia, each carrying two GBU-57A/B Massive Ordnance Penetrators (MOPs) — the largest conventional bomb in the US arsenal at ~$3.5M each. Tomahawk missiles were fired from a submarine simultaneously. The attack destroyed significant nuclear infrastructure and marked the first direct US military action against Iran. A single B-2 round-trip runs ~30 flight hours at ~$130,000/hr per USAF fact sheet.
14 GBU-57 MOPs: ~$49M · 7 B-2 sorties (~30 hr each): ~$27M · Tomahawks: est. $50–100M · Total day-1 est.: $150–300M
Wikipedia CSIS Analysis USAF B-2 Fact Sheet
September 27, 2025
UN sanctions snapback — comprehensive Iran nuclear sanctions reimposed
The UK, France, and Germany (the "E3") formally invoked the JCPOA UN snapback mechanism on August 28, 2025, citing Iran's "significant non-performance." After the mandatory 30-day notice period, UN sanctions were fully reimposed effective September 27, 2025. The US, which had already reimposed its own bilateral sanctions, welcomed the multilateral action. Elevated US military posture in the Persian Gulf continued; the US maintained 2 carrier strike groups on rotation in the region at a combined cost of approximately $13M/day in ship operations alone.
Ongoing theater baseline: $17–30M/day (pre-Epic Fury) · 2 CSG deployment: $13M/day in ship ops
US State Dept EU Council Wikipedia: Snapback
Late January 2026
Largest US Middle East naval buildup since 2003 — 2 carrier strike groups repositioned
USS Abraham Lincoln (CVN-72) and USS Gerald R. Ford (CVN-78) Carrier Strike Groups were both redirected to the Middle East, the largest US naval deployment to the region since the 2003 Iraq invasion. Each carrier strike group costs approximately $6.5M per day to operate in theater (ship fuel, maintenance, air wing hours, logistics). With both CSGs running simultaneously, the daily ship-operations cost alone reached ~$13M/day — before aircraft sorties, missile expenditures, or support logistics.
2 CSG daily operations: ~$13M/day ship costs · Theater total with forces: ~$40–80M/day
USNI News
February 28, 2026
Operation Epic Fury — 1,000+ Iranian targets hit in first 24 hours
The US and Israel launched coordinated operations "Epic Fury" (DoD) and "Roaring Lion" (IDF), striking over 1,000 Iranian military targets in the first 24 hours. Weapons included B-2 Spirit bombers from Diego Garcia, Tomahawk cruise missiles (~$2M each, current Block V procurement) from submarines and surface ships, HIMARS rockets, AI-guided loitering munitions (~$35,000 each), and F-35C sorties from carrier strike groups. Supreme Leader Khamenei was killed. Three US service members were killed in action in the first 24 hours.
Day-1 munitions estimate: $1–2B · Ongoing active operations rate: ~$200M/day

March 1, 2026
4th US service member killed; operations continue
A 4th US service member was killed in action. President Trump stated more casualties were "likely" while pledging to continue the operation, describing it as "not Iraq, not endless." US destroyers and submarines continued Tomahawk salvos targeting Iranian military and Revolutionary Guard infrastructure.
Cumulative 2-day operational cost estimate: $2–4B
March 2, 2026
Friendly fire: Kuwait downs 3 US F-15s · Hezbollah opens a third front
Three US Air Force F-15E Strike Eagles were shot down by Kuwaiti air defense systems in a friendly fire incident in the crowded operational airspace. All six aircrew ejected and were recovered in stable condition. CENTCOM acknowledged Kuwait's cooperation in the investigation. At current F-15EX replacement cost (~$94M per airframe), the loss represents ~$282M in aircraft alone, though original F-15E procurement costs were ~$31M in FY1998 dollars. Separately, Hezbollah opened a sustained assault on Israel's northern border, widening the conflict to a third active front.
3 F-15E airframes lost: ~$93M (original) / ~$282M (replacement value) · Total running: ~$16B
F-15E Wikipedia F-15EX Cost (Air Force Mag)

Counter mechanics: The running total is anchored at $16,000,000,000 as of March 2, 2026 (UTC midnight). It increments at $2,315 per second (~$200M/day), the estimated active combat operations rate since Operation Epic Fury began February 28, 2026. This rate reflects sustained Tomahawk salvos, carrier air wing operations, and logistics. For context: the Iraq War averaged ~$300–400M/day at its 2007 peak; the June 2025 Twelve-Day War peaked at $400–600M/day during the most intensive exchanges.

What is included: Direct US military operational costs; weapons, munitions, and air defense systems expended; FMF baseline appropriations; weapons drawdowns to Israel for Iran-conflict operations; Iran-proxy (Houthi) operation costs.

What is not included: Long-term veteran healthcare and disability costs (historically 30–40% of total war costs, per Brown University Costs of War Project); interest on war-related debt; full economic impact of oil market disruption (crude oil prices rose ~35% following Feb 28 strikes); intelligence operations; classified programs. The true total cost to taxpayers over 10–20 years will be substantially higher than what this counter reflects.
