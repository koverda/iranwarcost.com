## 1. Double-counting / Authorization vs Spending (legitster) — DEEP DIVE

**Criticism:** The $26.38B aid package is *authorized* spending, not immediately spent. CBO estimates disbursement through 2033. Then individual operations may overlap with that authorization — double-counting.

### Research findings

#### A. H.R.8034 is an APPROPRIATION, not just an authorization
The bill's full name is "Israel Security Supplemental **Appropriations** Act, 2024." This is actual budget authority, not merely an authorization. The money is legally committed.

#### B. CBO outlay timeline (from CBO score, April 2024)
Total budget authority: $26.38B (all provided in FY2024). Estimated **outlays** (actual cash out the door):

| Year | Defense ($13.04B BA) | State/ForeignOps ($12.94B BA) | Total |
|------|---------------------|-------------------------------|-------|
| 2024 | $2.98B | $4.12B | $7.10B |
| 2025 | $4.47B | $4.70B | $9.19B |
| 2026 | $2.12B | $2.79B | $4.99B |
| 2027 | $1.68B | $1.05B | $2.81B |
| 2028 | $0.91B | $0.24B | $1.24B |

So by end of FY2026 (Sep 2026), CBO estimated ~$21.3B of the $26.38B would be outlayed. By end of FY2028: ~$25.3B. The "through 2033" claim from legitster appears to be an overstatement — most money flows by 2028.

**However:** CBO made these estimates in April 2024, before the war escalated. Given active conflict, actual disbursement has almost certainly been *faster* than projected.

#### C. What's actually IN the $26.38B (Check Your Fact / PBS / CRS breakdown)
The $26.38B is NOT all military aid to Israel. Breakdown:

| Category | Amount | Actually for Israel military? |
|----------|--------|------------------------------|
| FMF (weapons procurement for Israel) | $3.5B | YES — direct to Israel |
| Missile defense (Iron Dome, David's Sling, Iron Beam) | $5.2B | YES — partly co-produced in US |
| Army ammunition procurement | $0.8B | YES — for Israel |
| Defense-wide O&M (US CENTCOM ops, force protection, deterrence) | $4.4B | NO — US operational costs |
| US CENTCOM region combat expenditure replacement | $2.4B | NO — US operational costs |
| International Disaster Assistance (humanitarian) | $5.66B | NO — Gaza humanitarian |
| Migration & Refugee Assistance (humanitarian) | $3.5B | NO — humanitarian |
| Other (USAID, State Dept operations) | ~$0.9B | NO — overhead |

**Key insight:** Only ~$9.5B of the $26.38B goes directly to Israel as military aid. Another ~$6.8B funds US military operations in the CENTCOM region. The remaining ~$9.15B is humanitarian aid for Gaza and refugees.

#### E. Brown University / Costs of War independent estimate
Brown's Costs of War project (Oct 2025) found:
- **$21.7B** in US military aid to Israel (Oct 2023–Sep 2025) — from ALL sources combined
- **$9.65–$12.07B** additional on wider Middle East ops (Yemen, deterrence, etc.)
- **Total: $31.4–$33.8B** through Sep 2025

This is an independent academic estimate of actual spending, not authorization. It validates that by Sep 2025 (before Epic Fury), real spending was already $31-34B. Our $44.9B figure through Mar 2026 (including Epic Fury's $4.3B and $1.9B equipment losses) is in the right ballpark.

#### F. The real double-counting risk

The double-counting is **less severe than initially thought** but still present:

1. **Item 2 ($0.6B)** — likely overlaps with H.R.8034's $5.2B missile defense line. **Double-counted.**
2. **Item 7 ($1.0B)** — partially overlaps with H.R.8034's FMF/drawdown provisions. **Partially double-counted.**
3. **Items 6 ($1.5B) and 8 ($2.0B)** — may partially overlap with H.R.8034's $6.8B in US ops funding. **Possibly double-counted.**
4. **Item 4 ($3.5B)** — THAAD/SM-3 interceptors fired by US forces are US assets funded from DoD procurement, NOT from Israel aid. This is likely **NOT double-counted** — H.R.8034's missile defense line funds Israeli systems (Iron Dome, David's Sling), not US THAAD.

**Estimated double-count: $1.5–4B** (mainly Items 2 and 7, possibly parts of 6 and 8)

#### G. The baseline FMF question (Item 3, $3.3B) — DETAILED RESEARCH

The $3.3B FMF in H.R.1968 is Israel's annual baseline under the 2016 MOU ($3.8B/yr = $3.3B FMF + $500M missile defense). H.R.1968 funded it at exactly the FY2024 base level — nothing extra was added. This $3.3B would have been appropriated in FY2025 regardless of the war.

**The H.R.8034 supplemental already includes $3.5B in EXTRA FMF** on top of the baseline. So the supplemental FMF is already counted in Item 1. The $3.3B baseline in Item 3 is a separate, pre-committed annual obligation.

**Case for removing ($41.6B):** The $3.3B is not incremental war spending. It's been appropriated annually since FY2019 under the MOU. Including it inflates the war cost by counting normal peacetime aid.

**Case for keeping ($44.9B):** Brown University's Costs of War project includes baseline FMF in their $21.7B total. The annual MOU aid IS being used to fight the war — it's not buying peacetime deterrence anymore. Most independent trackers (Brown, Quincy Institute) count it. The money is flowing to a country at war and directly funding that war effort.

**Decision needed from user:** This is a judgment call. Both positions are defensible. Removing it is more conservative and harder to attack; keeping it aligns with how independent academic trackers count.
